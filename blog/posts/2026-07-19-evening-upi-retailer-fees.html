The UPI Fee Debate: Should Large Retailers Pay for Digital Transactions?

July 19, 2026 · 3 min read

The Indian government is reportedly considering a significant policy shift: reintroducing a small Merchant Discount Rate (MDR) for large UPI retailers, as reported on July 18, 2026. This move, if implemented, would mark a departure from the current zero-MDR regime that has largely fueled UPI's explosive growth, sparking a crucial debate about the long-term sustainability of India's digital payments ecosystem versus its continued accessibility.

Why people say yes

Proponents argue that reintroducing MDR for large retailers is essential for the financial health of the UPI ecosystem. Banks and payment service providers incur substantial costs in maintaining, upgrading, and securing the UPI infrastructure, processing billions of transactions daily. The current zero-MDR policy, in place since January 2020, has meant these costs are largely absorbed by the government through incentives or by the providers themselves, which is seen as unsustainable. A small fee would provide a stable revenue stream to cover these operational expenses and ensure continued investment in the platform.

Many believe it's a matter of fair contribution. Large retailers benefit immensely from the speed, convenience, and widespread adoption of UPI, leading to increased digital transactions and reduced cash handling costs. Unlike small merchants, who may struggle with additional fees, large businesses with significant annual turnovers are better equipped to absorb these nominal charges. This differentiated approach, targeting only a small percentage of high-volume merchants, ensures that the burden doesn't fall on small enterprises or individual users, preserving UPI's inclusive character.

Furthermore, a sustainable revenue model could foster greater innovation and competition within the digital payments space. With assured income, payment companies and banks would have more resources to develop advanced features, enhance security measures, and expand UPI's reach into underserved areas. This, in turn, could lead to a more robust, reliable, and user-friendly payment system, ultimately benefiting the entire digital economy and solidifying India's position as a leader in real-time payments.

Why people say no

Opponents, however, raise concerns that even if directly levied on merchants, the cost of MDR could eventually be passed on to consumers. While government reports often state that consumers will remain unaffected, industry experts warn that retailers might recover these expenses through slightly higher product prices, by withdrawing discounts, or even by imposing subtle surcharges, effectively undermining UPI's cost-free appeal for the end-user. This could disproportionately affect everyday spending and consumer confidence in digital payments.

A key argument against reintroducing MDR is the potential to slow down the rapid adoption of digital payments in India. The zero-MDR policy was instrumental in making UPI ubiquitous, encouraging millions of small merchants and individuals to embrace cashless transactions. Introducing fees, especially for higher-value transactions, could create a psychological barrier, causing some large retailers to subtly discourage UPI usage or consumers to revert to cash for larger purchases, thereby hindering the nation's progress towards a less-cash economy.

Critics also emphasize UPI's role as a public digital infrastructure, akin to a utility. Charging for its use, even selectively, could compromise its foundational principle of being a free, accessible service for all. This shift could damage India's global reputation for pioneering free digital public goods and potentially complicate efforts to export the UPI model to other countries. Maintaining UPI's 'free' image is seen as crucial for its widespread trust and continued, unhindered expansion.

Key facts

  • The Indian government removed MDR on bank-to-bank UPI transactions in January 2020 to promote wider digital payment adoption.
  • The current proposal under consideration would apply to large merchants with an annual turnover exceeding ₹1-1.5 crore, likely only on UPI transactions above ₹2,000.
  • Proposed MDR rates for eligible transactions range from 5-7 basis points (0.05%-0.07%) to potentially up to 0.5%.

The bottom line

The debate over reintroducing MDR for large UPI retailers highlights a fundamental tension: balancing the need for a financially sustainable digital payments ecosystem with the desire to maintain UPI's widespread accessibility and affordability. While payment providers seek a stable revenue stream to cover escalating operational costs, concerns persist about potential impacts on consumer prices and the momentum of digital adoption. The government faces the complex task of finding a solution that nurtures the growth of UPI while ensuring its long-term viability without burdening its users.
