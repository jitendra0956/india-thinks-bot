NEET Leaks: Should India **Scrap** The Exam?

July 06, 2026 · 3 min read

India's National Eligibility cum Entrance Test (NEET), the gateway to medical careers, has once again faced a severe crisis. More than 2 million Indian students reportedly re-sat the medical entrance exam around June 21-22, 2026, following widespread allegations of paper leaks and marking errors. This incident has reignited a national debate on the integrity of the examination system and whether the NEET exam should be scrapped altogether. For millions of aspiring doctors, the recurring controversies surrounding this high-stakes test are a source of immense stress and uncertainty.

Why people say yes

Advocates for scrapping NEET argue that persistent paper leaks fundamentally undermine the credibility and fairness of the examination system. Such incidents invalidate the hard work of honest students, leading to a profound loss of trust in the entire academic process. Investigations into recent NEET-UG controversies have often revealed widespread organized crime networks, highlighting deep-seated systemic vulnerabilities that compromise the exam's integrity.

Furthermore, the recurring controversies and re-examinations inflict immense mental stress, anxiety, and financial burdens on students and their families. Many invest significant time and money in coaching, and the uncertainty caused by leaks creates severe emotional distress and can even lead to tragic outcomes. Critics also argue that NEET perpetuates an expensive coaching culture, disproportionately disadvantaging students from rural areas and low-income backgrounds.

Another significant concern is the perceived inequity in the examination's design. NEET's syllabus, largely aligned with the Central Board of Secondary Education (CBSE), is seen to disadvantage students from various state boards and rural areas who may lack access to similar educational resources or coaching. States like Tamil Nadu have consistently opposed NEET, asserting that it erodes state autonomy in education and deepens structural inequalities rather than promoting educational justice.

Why people say no

Opponents of scrapping NEET emphasize its crucial role in establishing a unified and standardized evaluation for medical admissions across India. Before NEET's introduction, students faced a multitude of state-level and private college entrance exams with varying difficulty levels and standards. NEET streamlined this process, ensuring a singular, merit-based selection that maintains a uniform quality standard for medical education nationwide.

Additionally, maintaining NEET is seen as a way to reduce the overall burden on aspiring medical students. It simplifies the admission process by replacing numerous individual entrance tests, thereby reducing confusion, travel costs, and application pressures. Proponents highlight that NEET has also been instrumental in curbing unfair practices, such as the demand for exorbitant capitation fees by private colleges, fostering a more transparent and accessible admission system based on merit.

Instead of abolishing NEET, many argue that the focus should be on implementing robust systemic reforms to address the issue of paper leaks. Suggestions include enhancing digital security, utilizing vast question banks, implementing multiple paper sets, and ensuring strict legal action against those involved in such organized crimes. Proponents believe that strengthening the examination agency, NTA, and improving logistical management are more effective solutions than discarding an essential national-level examination.

Key facts

  • NEET (National Eligibility cum Entrance Test) is the sole national entrance exam for admissions to MBBS, BDS, and various other undergraduate medical and allied health courses across India.
  • Annually, over 2 million students appear for the NEET exam, competing for approximately 1.1 to 1.3 lakh medical seats in government and private institutions.
  • Paper leaks are considered an organized crime in India, significantly undermining the integrity of competitive examination systems and impacting millions of aspirants.

The bottom line

The recent paper leak allegations have profoundly shaken confidence in the NEET examination system. The debate now centers on whether the foundational structure of NEET itself is flawed, demanding its abolition, or if targeted, comprehensive reforms can restore its integrity and ensure a fair pathway for all aspiring medical professionals in India.
