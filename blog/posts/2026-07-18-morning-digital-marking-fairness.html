India's Digital Exam Marking: A Fair Leap or Flawed System for Students?

July 18, 2026 · 3 min read

India's education landscape witnessed significant upheaval in May-June 2026, as the Central Board of Secondary Education (CBSE) rolled out its new On-Screen Marking (OSM) system for Class 12 exams. This digital shift, intended to modernize evaluations, quickly ignited a national outcry. Widespread student complaints about errors in grading, blurry scanned answer sheets, and technical glitches triggered protests across the country, leading to a Public Interest Litigation in the Supreme Court in July 2026. The controversy has sparked a critical debate: does digital marking truly enhance fairness and efficiency, or does its current implementation introduce new, detrimental challenges for students?

Why people say yes

Advocates for digital exam marking highlight its potential to streamline the evaluation process. By scanning answer sheets and having teachers assess them on computers, the system can significantly reduce manual totalling errors, ensuring greater accuracy in final scores. This technological approach promises increased transparency, as every step of the evaluation can be digitally recorded and monitored, theoretically making the process less susceptible to arbitrary decisions. Furthermore, digital marking can lead to faster processing of answer sheets and quicker declaration of results, which benefits students by reducing anxiety and speeding up university admissions. The system also aims for standardized marking, allowing for a more uniform evaluation across different examiners and regions, thereby potentially reducing human bias.

Why people say no

Critics argue that the implementation of digital marking has been deeply flawed, leading to significant unfairness for students. The most prominent issue has been widespread complaints of marking errors, with many students reporting lower-than-expected scores despite strong performance. Technical glitches have plagued the system, including blurred or incomplete scanned answer sheets, mismatched papers, and evaluators reportedly struggling with the new software. A crucial concern is the alleged lack of adequate training for evaluators, who were often figuring out the software while marking high-stakes exams. This unpreparedness undermined the system's accuracy and reliability. The gravity of these issues was underscored when the Supreme Court, in July 2026, expressed "serious concerns" over the system and the "frustration" it caused among students, demanding a response from the Centre.

Key facts

  • After Class 12 results in May 2026, over 400,000 students requested copies of their answer sheets due to widespread marking discrepancies linked to the new digital system.
  • The Supreme Court, in July 2026, raised "serious concerns" over the CBSE's On-Screen Marking system, noting student "frustration" and seeking a detailed response from the government.
  • A dry run conducted in January 2026 reportedly flagged major glitches and recommended delaying the system's implementation, warnings that were seemingly overlooked.

The bottom line

The introduction of digital exam marking in India represents a significant effort to modernize the examination system and enhance its efficiency and transparency. While the theoretical benefits are considerable, the initial rollout has been marred by substantial practical challenges and student distress. The ongoing debate highlights the critical need for robust testing, comprehensive training, and responsive technical support to ensure that technological advancements in education truly serve the best interests of students and uphold the integrity of the examination process.
