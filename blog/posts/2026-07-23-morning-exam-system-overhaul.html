India's Exam Integrity Crisis: Is an Overhaul the Only Answer?

July 23, 2026 · 3 min read

India's national examination system is once again under intense scrutiny, grappling with a crisis of integrity sparked by recurring paper leaks. The cancellation of the NEET UG 2026 exam due to alleged leaks, followed by a re-examination and an ongoing CBI probe, has fueled widespread student protests and prompted activist Sonam Wangchuk to undertake a hunger strike in July 2026, demanding systemic reforms. This escalating situation has ignited a nationwide debate: does India need a complete overhaul of its examination system, or can existing mechanisms be sufficiently strengthened to prevent future breaches?

Why people say yes

Proponents of a complete overhaul argue that paper leaks are not isolated incidents but a symptom of a deeply flawed system. Over 70 confirmed exam leaks have been reported across 15 states in the last seven years, affecting approximately 1.7 crore applicants. This recurring crisis points to fundamental vulnerabilities, including corruption and weak security protocols across the entire examination ecosystem, from paper setting to distribution. A radical restructuring is seen as necessary to address these systemic failures and restore the sanctity of competitive exams.

The current system is perceived to undermine meritocracy, leaving diligent students frustrated and fostering cynicism among the youth. The psychological stress on students, with many reportedly disillusioned and struggling to regain focus after cancellations, highlights the urgent need for change. An overhaul, they contend, could introduce genuinely student-centric processes, such as secure digital platforms and randomized question papers, ensuring fairness and rebuilding public trust in institutions that many now view as compromised.

Furthermore, advocates for an overhaul emphasize the need to embrace modern technology to combat leaks. Traditional paper-based exams are inherently susceptible to breaches. A comprehensive overhaul would integrate advanced solutions like end-to-end encryption for digital question papers, AI-powered proctoring for real-time monitoring, and blockchain technology for tamper-proof record-keeping. These technological advancements could create a more secure and transparent system, significantly reducing opportunities for malpractices.

Why people say no

Conversely, those against a complete overhaul argue that such a drastic measure would lead to massive disruption and impose substantial costs. A sweeping change would involve significant logistical challenges for millions of students and institutions across the country, potentially delaying academic cycles and creating further uncertainty. Re-examinations alone already incur considerable public funds and psychological burdens.

Instead of a full overhaul, many believe the focus should be on strengthening existing security measures and ensuring rigorous enforcement of current laws. India recently enacted the Public Examinations (Prevention of Unfair Means) Act, 2024, which introduces stringent penalties for paper leaks. Enhancing security protocols during printing, storage, and distribution, alongside stricter monitoring and accountability at every stage, could effectively curb malpractices without dismantling the entire system.

Moreover, the National Testing Agency (NTA), responsible for major national exams like NEET, is already undertaking significant reforms. These include a planned transition to Computer-Based Testing (CBT) to reduce physical handling vulnerabilities, a structural overhaul of the agency with dedicated data security verticals, and efforts to build a permanent, expert-driven staff. These ongoing initiatives demonstrate a commitment to improving the system, suggesting that targeted reforms rather than a complete overhaul might be a more practical and less disruptive path forward.

Key facts

  • Over 70 confirmed exam leaks have been reported across 15 states in the last seven years, affecting approximately 1.7 crore applicants.
  • The NEET UG 2026 exam, held on May 3, 2026, was cancelled on May 12, 2026, following investigations that revealed significant overlaps between a pre-circulated guess paper and the actual question paper.
  • The Public Examinations (Prevention of Unfair Means) Act, 2024, enacted in June 2024, mandates imprisonment of three to five years, and up to ten years for organized gangs, with fines up to ₹1 crore for offenses related to paper leaks.

The bottom line

The debate over overhauling India's national exam system reflects a fundamental tension between the urgent need for robust change to safeguard educational integrity and the practical complexities of implementing sweeping reforms. While the continuous stream of paper leaks has severely eroded public trust and student morale, there are ongoing efforts to strengthen the existing framework through legislative measures and technological upgrades. The path forward will require careful consideration to balance radical transformation with achievable improvements, all while addressing the anxieties and aspirations of millions of students.
