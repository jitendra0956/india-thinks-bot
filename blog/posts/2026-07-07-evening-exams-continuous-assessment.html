National Exams Under Fire: Should Continuous Assessment Be India's Future?

July 07, 2026 · 3 min read

The recent re-examination of the NEET exam on June 21, 2026, involving over 2 million Indian students due to an alleged paper leak, has reignited a critical debate about the future of national examinations in India. The incident, following a series of controversies including marking errors in CBSE exams, has exposed vulnerabilities in the current high-stakes testing system, causing immense stress and eroding public trust. This has prompted calls for a fundamental re-evaluation of how student learning is assessed, with many advocating for a shift towards continuous assessment.

Why people say yes

Advocates for continuous assessment argue that it significantly reduces the immense pressure and anxiety associated with high-stakes, single-event national exams. By evaluating students regularly through varied methods like quizzes, assignments, and projects, the burden is distributed, fostering a healthier learning environment. This approach allows for timely and targeted feedback, enabling teachers to identify and address learning gaps early, which leads to improved understanding and performance.

Continuous assessment promotes deeper learning and long-term retention, moving beyond the rote memorization often encouraged by summative exams. It encourages students to engage consistently with the material throughout the academic year. Furthermore, this method offers a more holistic evaluation of a student's abilities, assessing practical skills, critical thinking, problem-solving, and creativity, rather than just knowledge recall.

The National Education Policy (NEP) 2020 itself champions a transformation towards continuous, competency-based, and formative assessments, aiming to move away from high-stakes annual examinations. Proponents believe that replacing national exams with continuous assessment aligns perfectly with the NEP 2020's vision for a more student-centric and comprehensive education system.

Why people say no

Opponents argue that national examinations are crucial for maintaining standardization and fairness across India's vast and diverse educational landscape. They provide a uniform benchmark, allowing for objective comparison of student performance across different schools and regions, which is vital for higher education admissions and national accountability. Without a standardized national exam, ensuring consistent educational quality could become challenging.

Implementing continuous assessment on a national scale presents significant practical challenges, particularly the substantial increase in teacher workload. Teachers would require extensive training in new assessment techniques, test construction, and record-keeping, coupled with sufficient resources and smaller class sizes, which are often lacking in Indian schools. Previous attempts, such as the Continuous and Comprehensive Evaluation (CCE) program, faced difficulties due to uneven implementation and resource limitations.

Concerns also arise regarding the potential for inconsistency and subjectivity in grading if continuous assessment were the sole method of evaluation. Different teachers might apply varying criteria, leading to a lack of comparability and potentially undermining trust in the results. Furthermore, national exams often serve as a strong motivator for students to undertake comprehensive study of the entire syllabus, instilling discipline and preparing them for future academic and professional challenges.

Key facts

  • On June 21, 2026, over 2 million Indian students resat the NEET exam for undergraduate medical admissions following an alleged paper leak and reported irregularities in the original May 3rd examination.
  • India's National Education Policy (NEP) 2020 advocates for a shift from rote-based, high-stakes annual examinations towards continuous, competency-based, and formative assessments designed to guide learning rather than solely grade it.
  • The implementation of previous continuous assessment programs in India, like the Continuous and Comprehensive Evaluation (CCE), encountered challenges related to teacher preparedness, resource constraints, and a lack of consistent understanding among educators.

The bottom line

The debate over replacing national exams with continuous assessment in India is multifaceted, balancing the need for fair, reliable evaluation with concerns about student well-being and pedagogical effectiveness. While the recent controversies highlight the urgent need for reform in the existing examination system, a complete overhaul presents significant logistical and infrastructural hurdles. Any move towards a new assessment paradigm would require careful planning, extensive investment in teacher training and resources, and robust mechanisms to ensure standardization and fairness across the nation.
