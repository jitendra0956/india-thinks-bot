NEET Irregularities: Time for a **System** Overhaul?

July 11, 2026 · 3 min read

Recent reports detailing a week-long messaging app ban ahead of a NEET re-examination around July 4, 2026, amid climate activist Sonam Wangchuk's protest over alleged exam irregularities, have cast a fresh spotlight on India's crucial medical entrance test. The National Eligibility cum Entrance Test (NEET), a single nationwide examination for aspiring doctors, impacts millions annually. These escalating concerns have ignited a fervent national debate: do alleged irregularities warrant a complete system overhaul?

Why people say yes

Proponents for an overhaul argue that persistent paper leaks and malpractices have severely eroded public trust. The 2026 NEET-UG, initially held on May 3 for over 2.27 million aspirants, was cancelled on May 12 following investigations revealing significant overlaps between pre-circulated guess papers and the actual exam. This, alongside the 2024 NEET controversies involving leaks and grace marks, suggests systemic vulnerabilities, not isolated incidents. Critics contend that centralizing such a high-stakes exam creates a single point of failure, making it susceptible to organized rackets and undermining its integrity.

Moreover, the high-stakes nature of NEET inflicts immense psychological pressure on students, leading to widespread stress, anxiety, and tragically, reported suicides. Many argue the "one nation, one exam" model disadvantages rural, poor, and state board students by fueling an expensive urban coaching culture. This creates unequal access to medical education, exacerbating existing disparities.

Consequently, there are growing calls for fundamental structural changes. Demands include dissolving the National Testing Agency (NTA), criticized for its operational capacity and cybersecurity, or establishing a new, more accountable oversight body. Advocates believe that current defects are structural, requiring radical reform rather than superficial adjustments to restore fairness and public confidence.

Why people say no

Conversely, those opposing a complete overhaul highlight NEET's foundational goals: streamlining admissions by replacing multiple tests, standardizing merit, enhancing transparency, and curbing capitation fees in private institutions. They argue that despite its challenges, NEET provides a crucial uniform benchmark for national medical admissions, essential for consistent academic standards.

Furthermore, the government and NTA are actively implementing and proposing substantial reforms. These include the Public Examinations (Prevention of Unfair Means) Act, 2024, which criminalizes leaks. Technologically, a major shift to Computer-Based Testing (CBT) for NEET-UG from 2027 is planned, alongside advanced digital surveillance, AI-powered monitoring, multi-layer frisking, and Aadhaar-based biometric authentication. These are presented as robust, targeted solutions to enhance security and prevent future malpractices.

Opponents warn that a complete system overhaul risks plunging the medical admission process into greater instability and chaos. Scrapping the entire framework could negate efforts toward standardization and create new uncertainties for millions of aspiring students. They advocate for strengthening existing mechanisms, refining protocols, and gradually implementing recommended changes—like multi-stage examinations and increasing government MBBS seats—as a more pragmatic and less disruptive path forward.

Key facts

  • The NEET-UG 2026 examination, held on May 3, 2026, for over 2.27 million students, was cancelled on May 12, 2026, due to alleged paper leaks and irregularities, leading to a re-examination.
  • The National Testing Agency (NTA), responsible for conducting NEET, has faced criticism regarding its operational capacity, cybersecurity, and crisis communication following repeated controversies.
  • Significant reforms are planned, including a transition to Computer-Based Testing (CBT) for NEET-UG from 2027, enhanced digital surveillance, and stricter legal provisions against examination malpractice.

The bottom line

The alleged NEET exam irregularities in 2026 have undeniably brought to a head long-standing concerns about the fairness and integrity of India's medical entrance system. While student groups and various stakeholders demand a radical overhaul to restore trust and ensure equity, authorities are pushing for a combination of legislative action and technological upgrades to fortify the existing framework. The core of the debate lies in whether these incremental reforms are sufficient to address systemic vulnerabilities, or if the challenges demand a more fundamental restructuring of the "one nation, one exam" philosophy itself.
