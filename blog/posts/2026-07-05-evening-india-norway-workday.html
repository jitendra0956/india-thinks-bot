India's Workday Debate: Should We Follow Norway's 7.5-Hour Model?

July 05, 2026 · 3 min read

A viral LinkedIn post on July 3, 2026, ignited a nationwide discussion comparing India's demanding work culture with Norway's seemingly idyllic 7.5-hour workday. This comparison has sparked a crucial debate: should India consider adopting a shorter, more structured workday standard to combat burnout and enhance employee well-being, or would such a move hinder its economic growth and unique market dynamics?

Why people say yes

Proponents argue that a shorter workday, like Norway's 7.5-hour standard, could significantly improve work-life balance for Indian employees. With average weekly working hours often exceeding statutory limits, many workers in India face chronic stress and burnout, impacting their mental and physical health. A reduced workday could foster better personal relationships, allow for more leisure time, and promote overall well-being, leading to a happier and healthier workforce.

Furthermore, evidence suggests that shorter work hours do not necessarily equate to lower productivity. Several studies indicate that productivity often declines sharply after 50 hours per week, with additional hours yielding diminishing returns. Countries with shorter average working hours, such as Germany and Denmark, often exhibit higher hourly productivity. Adopting a more efficient, focused workday could therefore boost output quality and employee engagement, as seen in trials where reduced hours maintained or increased productivity.

A shift towards a more humane workday could also enhance India's appeal as a global talent hub. As younger generations increasingly prioritize work-life balance, companies offering better working conditions would be more attractive, helping to retain skilled professionals and draw in international talent. This could foster innovation and a more sustainable corporate culture, moving away from a "presenteeism" mindset that often glorifies long hours over actual output.

Why people say no

Opponents caution that India's economic context and developmental stage differ significantly from Norway's, making a direct adoption of its workday standard potentially detrimental. India is a developing economy with a large informal sector and unique labor market dynamics, where longer hours are often driven by economic necessities and the need to achieve livable earnings. Imposing a blanket 7.5-hour workday could risk slowing down economic growth and impacting global competitiveness, especially in industries that rely on high output volume.

Moreover, critics argue that a mandatory reduction in working hours could lead to unintended consequences such as increased part-time employment with potentially lower wages and fewer benefits, or compel workers to take on multiple jobs to meet financial needs, thereby increasing stress rather than reducing it. Some research suggests that while shorter workweeks are appealing, they haven't consistently demonstrated long-term productivity increases and could negatively impact a country's GDP.

Culturally, India's work ethic often associates long hours with dedication and commitment, especially in competitive sectors like IT and finance. A sudden, top-down imposition of shorter hours might face resistance from both employers and employees who perceive it as a barrier to career progression or economic advancement. Significant structural and cultural shifts would be required, which could be complex and challenging to implement across diverse industries and regions.

Key facts

  • Norway's standard full-time work week is typically 37.5 hours, or 7.5 hours per day, often including an unpaid 30-minute lunch break.
  • Indian workers average 45.7 hours per week, with statutory limits typically set at 48 hours per week or 9 hours per day.
  • A significant 59% of Indian employees report experiencing symptoms of burnout, ranking among the highest globally.

The bottom line

The debate over adopting Norway's 7.5-hour workday in India highlights a tension between employee well-being and economic realities. While a shorter workday offers clear benefits in terms of reducing burnout and potentially boosting per-hour productivity, its feasibility and impact within India's distinct economic and social landscape require careful consideration. Any potential reform would need to weigh the aspirations for a healthier workforce against the practical challenges of implementation and its effects on national output and employment structures.
