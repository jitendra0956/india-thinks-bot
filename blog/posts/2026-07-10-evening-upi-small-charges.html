The Great UPI Debate: Should Small Transactions Carry a Fee?

July 10, 2026 · 3 min read

The National Payments Corporation of India (NPCI) recently initiated a pilot program to introduce nominal transaction charges on UPI payments below ₹100, aiming to cover the operational costs of the robust digital payment system. This move has reignited a critical debate across India: should the convenience and widespread adoption of UPI, particularly for micro-transactions, come with a price tag? The outcome of this pilot could significantly reshape India's digital payment landscape, impacting millions of users and small businesses who rely on UPI daily.

Why people say yes

Advocates for imposing charges argue that a fee, even a nominal one, is crucial for the long-term sustainability of the UPI ecosystem. While the NPCI itself operates efficiently, processing billions of transactions, the broader infrastructure—including banks and payment service providers (PSPs)—incurs significant costs for maintenance, cybersecurity, fraud detection, and customer support. Government subsidies currently only cover a fraction of these operational expenses, leading to a substantial funding gap for industry players.

Introducing charges could also incentivize further innovation and investment within the digital payments space. With a stable revenue stream, banks and fintech companies would have more resources to develop new features, enhance security, and expand UPI's reach into underserved areas. Without a clear cost-recovery mechanism, the financial strain on these entities threatens to hinder growth and limit the evolution of the platform.

Furthermore, proponents suggest that UPI, like other payment systems such as credit cards, IMPS, and NEFT, should have a revenue model that reflects its operational demands. A tiered approach, where small transactions might incur a very minimal fee or remain free while larger or commercial transactions are charged, is often cited as a balanced solution. This could ensure that those who benefit most from the system contribute to its upkeep, without unduly burdening the most vulnerable users.

Why people say no

Opponents strongly contend that imposing charges on small UPI transactions would severely undermine financial inclusion, a cornerstone of UPI's success. The zero-cost nature of UPI has been instrumental in bringing millions of low-income and rural users into the digital economy, reducing reliance on cash. Introducing fees could deter these users, potentially pushing them back to less formal or cash-based transactions, thereby reversing years of progress.

Public sentiment overwhelmingly favors keeping UPI free. Surveys indicate that a significant majority, up to 75% of users, might reduce or completely stop using UPI if transaction fees are implemented, even if nominal. This price sensitivity, particularly in a high-frequency, low-ticket value ecosystem like India's, highlights the risk of widespread user exodus and a significant drop in digital payment volumes.

Many view UPI as a public utility and a national asset that offers substantial societal and economic benefits beyond direct revenue. It reduces the costs associated with printing and handling cash, increases transparency, and improves tax compliance. From this perspective, the government and the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) should continue to bear the costs, perhaps through subsidies or by having banks absorb them, given the broader economic advantages derived from a robust and free digital payment infrastructure.

Key facts

  • A parliamentary committee has recommended tiered charges for UPI transactions to address a significant funding gap for ecosystem players.
  • Surveys show 70-75% of users may stop using UPI if transaction fees are introduced.
  • The average UPI transaction value has decreased, underscoring its use for small, everyday purchases.

The bottom line

The debate over UPI charges for small transactions balances the critical need for ecosystem sustainability against the imperative of financial inclusion and widespread public adoption. While operational costs are a genuine concern for payment providers, the potential impact on user behavior and the foundational principle of UPI as a digital public good cannot be overlooked. Finding a balanced solution that ensures the long-term viability of UPI without alienating its vast user base remains a complex challenge for policymakers and stakeholders alike.
