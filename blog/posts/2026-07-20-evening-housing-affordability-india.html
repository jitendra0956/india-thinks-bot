India's Housing Dilemma: Is Homeownership Becoming an Impossible Dream?

July 20, 2026 · 3 min read

A Noida resident's recent viral video, lamenting an ₹80 lakh 'matchbox-sized flat,' has reignited a heated national debate: are Indian housing prices now widely unaffordable for ordinary citizens? The incident on July 20, 2026, quickly spread, echoing a growing sentiment among many aspiring homeowners grappling with the soaring costs of real estate in India's bustling urban centers. This viral moment underscores a critical question about the accessibility of housing in a rapidly developing economy.

Why people say yes

Many argue that housing prices have surged disproportionately compared to average incomes, especially in major metropolitan areas. In the first quarter of 2026, housing prices in eight major Indian cities saw an annual increase of 3-24%, with Bengaluru experiencing a 24% rise, and Mumbai Metropolitan Region (MMR) and Delhi-NCR growing by 20% and 18% respectively. This rapid appreciation, coupled with stagnant or moderately increasing salaries, means that what once required 5-6 years of savings now demands 10+ years of disciplined financial planning.

Furthermore, there's a significant decline in the supply of affordable housing units. Reports indicate that the share of affordable housing (homes priced below ₹40-50 lakh) in new launches has plummeted. From 38% in 2019, it dropped to just 18% in 2025, and only 10% in Q1 2026. Developers increasingly prioritize high-margin luxury projects, which offer profit margins of 25-30%, compared to 10-12% for affordable housing, further limiting options for ordinary citizens. The Mumbai Metropolitan Region (MMR) and National Capital Region (NCR) are explicitly noted to be above the 50% affordability threshold, indicating significant financial pressure for households looking to buy in these areas.

Rising loan EMIs and interest rates also contribute to the unaffordability crisis. Even with some moderation in interest rates, the sheer increase in property values leads to higher monthly burdens, longer loan tenures, and increased financial stress for many families. The proportion of household income required to service monthly home loan EMIs has increased significantly for middle-income buyers.

Why people say no

Conversely, some analyses suggest a more nuanced picture, pointing to improving affordability over the past decade. A Colliers India report from December 2025 indicated that the Price-to-Income ratio has nearly halved since 2010, moving from 88.5 to 45.3 in 2025, largely because average incomes have grown faster than housing prices. Looking ahead, the CBRE Housing Affordability Index (March 2026) projects that home affordability in India's top cities is expected to stabilize between 2026 and 2028, with household income growth anticipated to outpace property price appreciation for the first time since 2021.

Government initiatives, particularly the Pradhan Mantri Awas Yojana-Urban (PMAY-U 2.0), play a crucial role in making homeownership more accessible. Launched in September 2024 and active until 2029, PMAY-U 2.0 aims to sanction houses for an additional one crore urban beneficiaries, providing financial support through interest subsidies and partnerships. As of February 2026, over 13.61 lakh houses have been sanctioned under this scheme, with consistent efforts to expand its reach. These schemes, along with tax benefits and incentives for affordable housing, aim to reduce the financial burden on buyers.

Moreover, lower borrowing costs due to the Reserve Bank of India's (RBI) monetary easing have also supported homebuyer affordability. The cumulative impact of 125 basis points of rate cuts and a stable interest-rate environment since early 2023 have made home loans more manageable. Beyond the major metros, Tier 2 and Tier 3 cities are emerging as more affordable alternatives. These cities are witnessing significant infrastructure development and employment growth, making them attractive for homebuyers seeking lower property costs. While land prices in these areas are projected to rise, they still offer relatively cheaper options compared to the saturated Tier 1 cities.

Key facts

  • Housing prices in India's eight major cities increased by 3-24% annually in Q1 2026, with Bengaluru seeing the highest appreciation at 24%.
  • The Mumbai Metropolitan Region (MMR) and National Capital Region (NCR) remain above the 50% affordability threshold, meaning more than half of household income is needed for EMI.
  • The share of affordable housing (below ₹40-50 lakh) in new launches dropped from 38% in 2019 to 10% in Q1 2026.

The bottom line

The debate over housing affordability in India is complex, reflecting a market in transition. While soaring prices in prime metropolitan areas and a shrinking supply of affordable homes present significant challenges for many, government policies, stabilizing income-to-EMI ratios, and the growth of Tier 2 and Tier 3 cities offer alternative pathways to homeownership. The overall picture suggests a bifurcated market, where premium segments thrive while efforts continue to make housing accessible for a broader segment of the population.
