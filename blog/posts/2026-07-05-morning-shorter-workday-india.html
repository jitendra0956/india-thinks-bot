India's Workday Debate: Shorten Hours or Stay the Course?

July 05, 2026 · 3 min read

The recent viral LinkedIn post comparing India's demanding work culture to Norway's 7.5-hour workday has ignited a crucial debate: Should Indian companies embrace a shorter workday? With reports highlighting India as one of the most overworked nations globally and a significant portion of its workforce experiencing burnout, the question of adopting reduced working hours is more pertinent than ever, prompting a re-evaluation of traditional work paradigms.

Why people say yes

Proponents argue that a shorter workday can significantly enhance employee well-being. Reduced hours often lead to lower stress levels, decreased burnout, and improved mental and physical health, allowing employees more time for personal pursuits and rest. This, in turn, can foster a healthier and more engaged workforce. Studies in countries like the UK and New Zealand have shown that employees experience less anxiety and fatigue with shorter weeks.

A compressed or shorter workweek has also been linked to a boost in productivity and focus. When employees have fewer hours, they tend to become more efficient, prioritize tasks better, and eliminate time-wasting activities, leading to the same or even higher output. Microsoft Japan, for instance, reported a 40% increase in productivity during a trial of a four-day workweek. The underlying principle is working smarter, not just longer.

Furthermore, adopting a shorter workday culture can be a powerful tool for talent attraction and retention in a competitive global market. Companies offering improved work-life balance are more appealing to potential employees, especially younger generations who increasingly prioritize flexibility. This can lead to reduced employee turnover, saving companies significant hiring and training costs.

Why people say no

Critics express concerns that shortening workdays could lead to a reduction in overall output and negatively impact economic growth. In a country like India, where long hours are often equated with commitment and productivity, a shift to fewer hours might be perceived as a decrease in effort, potentially slowing down project delivery and competitive edge. This is particularly relevant given India's ambition to be a global economic powerhouse.

Another significant concern is the potential for increased operational costs for businesses. While some savings might occur from reduced office utility usage, the core cost of salaries and benefits typically remains the same for fewer working hours. If the workload isn't effectively managed, companies might need to hire more staff to cover the same amount of work, thereby increasing labor expenses.

Finally, maintaining existing client expectations can be challenging with shorter workdays, especially for businesses with global operations or those in service-oriented sectors. Clients often expect constant availability and quick turnarounds, which might be difficult to uphold if a company's working hours are significantly reduced. The Indian work culture, often characterized by "always-on" connectivity, could face resistance to this change.

Key facts

  • Indian workers log an average of 45.7 to 46.7 hours per week, making India one of the most overworked nations globally.
  • Up to 78% of employees in India experience job burnout, with some surveys indicating figures as high as 83% in the IT sector.
  • Norway's standard full-time workweek is 37.5 hours (7.5 hours/day), with the average weekly hours being around 33.6, among the shortest globally.

The bottom line

The debate over adopting a shorter workday culture in India is complex, balancing the clear benefits of improved employee well-being and potential productivity gains against concerns about economic impact and deeply ingrained cultural norms. While global trials demonstrate positive outcomes in terms of employee satisfaction and efficiency, India's unique labor laws, which currently permit compressed workweeks (up to 12 hours a day for a 4-day week) rather relevant than reduced total hours, present a distinct set of challenges. A successful transition would require careful consideration of industry-specific needs, a shift towards outcome-based performance metrics, and a concerted effort to redefine the perception of work and productivity in the Indian context.
