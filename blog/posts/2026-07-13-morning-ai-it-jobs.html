AI's Dual Impact: Threat or Opportunity for Indian IT?

July 13, 2026 · 3 min read

Last week, reports highlighted a concerning trend in India's IT sector: "silent layoffs," with estimates suggesting 25,000 to 35,000 jobs could be lost in 2026 due to AI automation and restructuring. This news has reignited a crucial debate: Is artificial intelligence a formidable threat to Indian IT jobs, or does it usher in a new era of opportunities through re-skilling and new roles? The nation is deliberating the long-term implications for its vibrant technology workforce.

Why people say yes

Concerns about job displacement are significant, with industry estimates pointing to substantial job losses in the Indian IT sector. Staffing firms project that between 25,000 and 35,000 technology professionals could lose their jobs through "silent layoffs" in 2026 alone, with some cumulative estimates for 2025 and 2026 reaching as much as 43,000. These reductions are often attributed to AI-driven automation, cost-cutting measures, and shifts towards competency-based organizational structures, impacting traditional roles and middle management.

AI's increasing capability to automate repetitive or process-driven functions means many traditional IT tasks are becoming redundant. This structural shift reduces the need for a large workforce focused on routine coding, documentation, or basic support. Companies are streamlining operations, eliminating overlapping roles, and accelerating automation, pushing employees out through performance reviews and skill relevance rather than formal announcements.

The overall landscape of IT employment is also showing signs of contraction in traditional areas. While AI-specific hiring is surging, overall IT job listings have seen a decline. For instance, in June 2026, AI hiring in India's IT sector rose by 16%, but overall IT job listings fell by 3% year-on-year. This divergence suggests a shrinking market for generalist IT roles, placing pressure on professionals without specialized, future-ready skills.

Why people say no

Despite the concerns, many argue that AI is primarily a catalyst for transformation, not widespread job destruction. The rapid adoption of AI is creating a significant surge in demand for new, specialized roles. In June 2026, AI hiring in India's IT sector saw a 16% rise, outpacing the overall IT recruitment decline. Roles such as Machine Learning Engineers, Data Scientists, Generative AI Specialists, and Prompt Engineers are in high demand across various industries.

Companies and the government are actively investing in massive re-skilling and up-skilling initiatives to prepare the workforce for this AI-driven future. Major firms like TCS and Accenture are leveraging their India operations to build AI solutions and are investing in training programs. Databricks has also committed over $250 million to India to advance data and AI innovation, including launching an Academy to train 500,000 individuals over three years. These efforts aim to bridge the talent gap, as India needs an estimated 1 million AI professionals by 2026, but currently only has about 100,000 qualified candidates.

Furthermore, AI is viewed as a tool to enhance human productivity and create higher-value work, rather than simply replacing workers. Experts suggest AI will shift many roles toward activities requiring human judgment, problem-solving, and creative thinking. This transformation can lead to greater operational efficiency and economic growth, positioning India as a global hub for AI services and talent. The focus is on transitioning the workforce from "AI illiterate" to "AI literate" roles.

Key facts

  • Reports indicate that India's IT sector could see between 25,000 and 35,000 job losses in 2026 due to AI automation and restructuring.
  • AI hiring in India's IT sector rose by 16% in June 2026, while overall IT job listings declined by 3% in the same period, signaling a shift in demand.
  • India is projected to need 1 million AI professionals by 2026, but currently possesses only about 100,000 qualified candidates, indicating a significant skill gap.

The bottom line

The emergence of AI automation presents a complex challenge and opportunity for India's IT sector. While some traditional jobs face displacement due to increased efficiency and automation, there is a clear and growing demand for specialized AI skills. The future of Indian IT employment will likely depend on the industry's ability to adapt, invest in re-skilling its vast workforce, and pivot towards higher-value AI-driven services and innovative roles. This transition is not about eliminating work but redefining its nature within a rapidly evolving global technological landscape.
